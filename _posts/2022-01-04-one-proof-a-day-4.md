This is from one of the assignments of the modern algebra course I took last semester.

**Question**\
If $$m$$ is a positive integer, we let $$\varphi$$ be the Euler's $$\varphi$$-function. Now prove that
$$\sum_{m>0, \, m | n} \varphi(m) = n$$
where $$\varphi(m)$$ is the number of generators of $$C_m$$ which is a cyclic group with $$m$$ elements.

*Solution*\
Let us define a relation $$\sim$$: $$g \sim h$$ by $$\langle g\rangle = \langle h\rangle$$ for $$g, h \in G$$. Then $$\sim$$ is an equivalence relation. First, we have $$g \sim g$$ for all $$g\in G$$ because $$\langle g\rangle = \langle g\rangle$$. Second, we have $$g \sim h \Leftrightarrow  \langle g\rangle = \langle h\rangle \Leftrightarrow h \sim g$$. Lastly, if $$g \sim h, h \sim k$$, then $$\langle g\rangle = \langle h\rangle =  \langle k\rangle$$ hence $$g \sim k$$.

We can consider partitions of $$C_n$$ by $$\sim$$. Denote $$P = C_n/\sim$$. Since $$P$$ is a partition of a finite set, we can say that $$\sum_{p\in P} \vert p\vert = \vert C_n\vert = n$$.

(1) All groups generated by one element of $$G$$ is a subgroup of $$G$$. And they're all isomorphic to $$C_m$$ for some $$m\vert n$$.

(2) For any $$m$$ such that $$m \vert n$$, $$C_m$$ is isomorphic to $$\langle n/m\rangle$$.

By (1), (2), we can say that there exists $$p \in P$$ for all $$m \vert n$$ such that $$C_m$$ is isomorphic to $$\langle g\rangle$$ for $$g\in p$$. And there exists $$m$$ such that $$ m \vert n$$ for all $$p \in P$$ such that $$C_m$$ is isomorphic to $$\langle g\rangle$$ for some $$g\in p$$.
For $$m, p, g$$ in such relation,

$$number \ of \ generators \ of \ C_m$$
$$= number \ of \ generators \ of \ \langle g\rangle = \vert p\vert$$.

Therefore
$$\sum_{m>0, \, m | n} \varphi(m) = \sum_{p\in P} |p| = n$$
